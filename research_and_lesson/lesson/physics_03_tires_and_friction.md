# Physics 03: Tires, Slip, and Friction

## Objective

Understand how tire-road interaction affects longitudinal force generation.

## Core Idea

The tire is the interface between the vehicle and the road. Even if the engine can produce torque, the vehicle cannot accelerate unless the tire can transmit force through friction.

## Tire Slip

Slip occurs when wheel rotation and vehicle ground speed do not perfectly match. This becomes important during strong acceleration or braking.

- Small slip can help generate force.
- Excessive slip reduces controllability and may reduce usable traction.

## ASCII Sketch

```text
Pure rolling idea:

wheel rotation  ~=  vehicle ground speed

High-slip idea:

wheel rotation  !=  vehicle ground speed
```

In strong acceleration, the wheel may spin faster than the vehicle advances. In strong braking, the wheel may rotate much more slowly than the vehicle speed would suggest.

## Friction

The available tire force depends on road-tire friction. Dry asphalt, wet pavement, and loose surfaces can produce very different levels of grip.

## Pure Rolling Assumption

Many simple models assume pure rolling, meaning the tire rolls without meaningful slip. That assumption is useful for simplicity, but it becomes inaccurate during aggressive acceleration, braking, or low-friction conditions.

## Why It Matters

Ignoring slip can produce unrealistic predictions for acceleration, braking distance, and energy usage. Including it improves physical realism.

## Example Problems

### Problem 1

Why can a car fail to accelerate strongly on a wet road even if the engine produces high torque?

Answer:
Because the available tire-road friction is lower, the tire may slip before it can transmit the full driving force to the road.

### Problem 2

Why does a pure rolling assumption become weaker during hard braking?

Answer:
Hard braking increases the chance of wheel slip, so the wheel speed and vehicle ground speed no longer match closely.
